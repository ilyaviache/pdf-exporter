The properties of fractal speckle-like images are examined in the context of their use in art therapy and ophthalmology. Their construction utilized both the traditional approach based on two-dimensional fractal functions and a new method based on dynamic chaos concepts. The important role of scaling characteristics of Fourier images of fractal light structures is revealed.

INTRODUCTION

Methods of fractal physics have found application in the analysis and construction of various spatial-temporal structures (see, for example [1]). Considerable attention is paid to the development and research of fractal light fields, which are widely used in various devices. For instance, wave beams formed by passing a plane wave through a plate with a Cantor transmission profile ensure multiple focusing of radiation during propagation [2]. Fractal vortex beams, due to their high degree of resistance to turbulent heterogeneities, have found use in atmospheric communication lines [3].
Recently, interest in studying fractal speckle-like structures has intensified. There has been a whole range of evidence pointing to positive examples of their use in art therapy (art therapy), medical and biological research [6], light therapy [7], and ophthalmology [8-10]. Analysis of the literature indicates that the effectiveness of using light structures in art therapy, light therapy, and ophthalmology significantly depends on the presence of fractal characteristics, regardless of the overall texture of their amplitude-phase distribution. Attention is drawn to the first-noted fact in [8] of improving the patient's psychological state during the elimination of eye pathology. In [7], data on reducing anxiety levels, unloading perception systems, and positive
dynamics of the influence of fractal laser visual dynamic images on the human functional state were obtained using electroencephalography methods. The indicated connection between fractal therapy methods in ophthalmology and art therapy can be explained by the features of optical signal processing in the cerebral cortex. The key point determining this connection is that the Fourier images of fractal light fields formed during optical signal processing in the cerebral cortex also possess a fractal structure [11]. Due to this, low and high spatial frequencies are processed in the neuronal network of the cortex using the same algorithm, which improves the perception of optical images and enhances the connection between the involved groups of neurons. Under such conditions, the patient experiences a sense of comfort associated with easier perception of image features.
Methods for generating such light fields most often use algorithms based on the properties of deterministic and random functions [11] with fractal properties. Recently, there have been reports of the possibility of using models based on the concepts of dynamic chaos [12]. The purpose of this work is to establish to what extent the difference in approaches to forming optical fractal images and the scatter of parameters used can affect the manifestation and relationship of scaling properties of images and their spatial spectra.
